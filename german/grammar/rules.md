# Grammar — Rules

*Apply these by default. Demote to hypotheses.md if contradicted by new data.*
*Source: Hammer's German Grammar and Usage, 5th Ed.*

---

## Nouns: Gender

### By Ending (most reliable for production)
- All nouns in **-chen** and **-lein** are NEUTER — no exceptions: das Mädchen, das Büchlein, das Fräulein
- All nouns in **-ung, -heit, -keit, -schaft, -tät, -tion, -sion, -ur, -ik, -ei** are FEMININE
- All nouns in **-ismus, -ling, -or, -us, -ant, -ast** are MASCULINE
- All nouns in **-tum, -chen, -lein, -ma (Latin), -ment, -um** are NEUTER
- Nouns from verb infinitives used as nouns are always NEUTER: das Essen, das Schreiben, das Kommen
- Nouns in **-er from verbs** are always MASCULINE: der Bäcker, der Lehrer, der Bohrer

### By Meaning (quick checks)
- Seasons, months, days of the week → MASCULINE (exceptions: die Nacht, die Woche, das Jahr)
- Alcoholic and plant-based drinks → MASCULINE (exception: das Bier)
- Makes of car → MASCULINE
- Rivers outside Germany → MASCULINE (those ending -a/-e are feminine)
- Native German rivers → FEMININE (exceptions: der Inn, Lech, Main, Neckar, Rhein)
- Young animals and children → NEUTER: das Baby, das Kind, das Kalb
- Metals and chemical elements → NEUTER (exceptions: die Bronze, der Stahl, der Schwefel)
- Countries/continents/towns → NEUTER, used without article unless adjective present (exceptions: die Schweiz, die Türkei, der Irak, etc.)

### Compound Nouns
- Compound nouns always take the gender of their **last component**
  - der Fahrplan, die Bushaltestelle, das Hallenbad
  - Exception: compounds of *Mut* → feminine: die Demut, die Armut, die Schwermut

### Abbreviations and Shortenings
- Abbreviations take the gender of their base word: die CDU, der HSV
- Shortened words keep the gender of the full form: die Uni, der Akku

## Nouns: Plurals

### Feminine plurals
- Over 90% of feminine nouns form the plural in **-en/-n** — default to this when unsure
  - Exception: die Mutter→Mütter, die Tochter→Töchter (only two feminines with ¨ plural)
  - Nouns in -in double the consonant: die Ärztin→Ärztinnen
  - NO feminine nouns have plurals in (-) or (¨er)

### Masculine plurals
- Default for most masculine nouns: **(-e)** or **(¨e)**
- Masculine nouns ending in **-el, -en, -er**: no plural ending (die Lehrer, die Onkel) — BUT learn the ~20 that umlaut (Äpfel, Brüder, Väter, Vögel, Böden, Gärten, etc.)

### Neuter plurals
- Neuter nouns ending in **-el, -en, -er**, diminutives in **-chen/-lein**, and **Ge…e** words: plural without ending: das Segel→Segel, das Mädchen→Mädchen

### -s plural
- **Recent loan words** from English/French: default to -s: das Hotel→Hotels, das Team→Teams
- English words in -y → plural -**ys** (NOT -ies): die Babys, die Rowdys

### Nouns in -mann
- As occupation/group → **-leute**: der Kaufmann→Kaufleute, der Fachmann→Fachleute
- As individual men → **-männer**: Schneemänner, Ehrenmänner

### Measurement nouns
- Masculine and neuter measurement nouns stay **singular** after numerals: drei Glas Bier, zwei Pfund Kirschen
- Feminine measurement nouns take the **plural**: vier Tassen Kaffee, zehn Flaschen Wein
- *die Mark* never takes a plural ending

### Body parts / clothing
- When each person has one, use the **singular** in German (where English uses plural):
  - Alle hoben die rechte Hand — They all raised their right hands

### Key double-gender pairs to memorise
- **der See** (lake) / **die See** (sea)
- **der Leiter** (leader) / **die Leiter** (ladder)
- **das Messer** (knife) / **der Messer** (gauge)
- **das Gehalt** (salary) / **der Gehalt** (content)
- **die Mutter** (mother) / **die Muttern** (nuts/bolts — different plural too)
- **die Wörter** (individual words) / **die Worte** (connected words, sayings)
- **die Länder** (countries) / **die Lande** (regions, historical)

## Nouns: Declension

### Case endings on the noun itself
- Genitive singular (masc./neut.): add **-(e)s** — *des Vaters, des Kindes, des Jahres*
  - Monosyllables prefer -es; polysyllables prefer -s
  - MUST use -es after: -s, -ß, -x, -z (des Krebses, des Maßes, des Kreuzes)
- Dative plural: ALL nouns add **-n** unless plural already ends in -n or -s
  - den Männer**n**, den Jahr**en** — but den Frauen (already -n), den Autos (ends in -s)

### 'Weak' masculine nouns
- Add **-n or -en** in ALL cases except nominative singular: den Studenten, des Studenten, dem Studenten
- Without a determiner: NO ending (Die Situation war für Arzt und Patient kritisch)
- Exception: **Herr** always keeps -n in singular even without determiner: *Herrn Alfred Bletzer*
- 8 irregular masculines have **-ns** in genitive singular (not -n): des Name**ns**, des Gedanke**ns**

## Cases

### Nominative
- Predicate complement of *sein, werden, bleiben, heißen, scheinen* → NOMINATIVE: Karl ist **mein Freund**

### Accusative
- Conventional greetings/wishes are ACCUSATIVE: **Guten** Morgen, **Herzlichen** Glückwunsch, **Besten** Dank
- *bis, durch, für, gegen, ohne, um* → ALWAYS accusative
- *an, auf, hinter, in, neben, über, unter, vor, zwischen* → accusative when expressing **directed movement**

### Genitive
- Genitive noun phrase FOLLOWS the noun it depends on — EXCEPT proper names come first: **Karls** Freund
- In colloquial speech: replace genitive with **von** + dative (except with proper names)
- *von* MUST be used (even in writing) when the noun stands alone: der Bau von Kraftwerken
- *von* MUST be used with personal pronouns (not genitive): fünf **von euch**, ein Freund **von ihr**
- Genitive prepositions: *innerhalb, statt, trotz, während, wegen*

### Dative
- These verbs take DATIVE (not accusative) object: danken, dienen, folgen, gefallen, gehören, gehorchen, gratulieren, helfen, nützen, passen, raten, schaden, schmeicheln, vertrauen, widersprechen, zuhören
- **Never say *Ich bin kalt*** — use dative construction: *Mir ist kalt* / *Es ist mir kalt*
  - Same for: bange, gut, heiß, schlecht, schwindlig, übel, (un)wohl, warm
- Dative of possession with body parts:
  - With prepositions (not *mit*): MUST use dative: **Mir** fiel die Mütze vom Kopf
  - When referring to someone else's body part: MUST use dative: Die Mutter wäscht **ihm** die Hände
  - When obvious/reflexive and no preposition: NO dative needed: Er machte die Augen auf

### Apposition
- Apposition takes the SAME CASE as the noun it follows
- Geographic names: apposition, not genitive: die Universität **Hamburg** (not *Hamburgs*)
- Battles: *bei* not *von*: die Schlacht **bei** Lützen

## Pronouns: Personal

### Genitive of Personal Pronouns
- Only use in very formal written registers — replace in practice:
  - With verbs: use alternative construction
  - After most prepositions: use **dative** (*wegen uns, trotz ihm*)
  - Partitive and other contexts: use **von**: *sechs von ihnen, ein Freund von mir*
- Genitive personal pronouns refer to persons/animals; for things use demonstratives *dessen/deren*
- Special wegen/willen/halben forms: **meinetwegen, deinetwegen, um ihretwillen, seinethalben**

### Reflexive Pronouns
- *sich* for 3rd person (all genders, both numbers) and polite *Sie* — accusative and dative
- After a preposition, reflexive refers back to the **subject** of the sentence: *Er hatte kein Geld bei sich*
- In infinitive constructions **without** *zu*: reflexive → refers to **object** of finite verb; non-reflexive → refers to subject
- In infinitive clauses **with** *zu*: pronoun choice depends on who is subject of infinitive
- *selbst/selber* for emphatic 'myself/yourself' etc. (stressed); unstressed *selbst* = 'even'
- Reciprocal: use *einander* (not *sich*) **after prepositions**: *voneinander, miteinander*

### Prepositional Adverb vs. Pronoun
- *es* is NEVER used after prepositions → use *da(r)+preposition* instead
- *Preposition + pronoun* for **individual persons**; *da(r)+prep* for things, abstracts, groups, whole sentences
- **Four prepositions with NO prepositional adverb**: *außer, gegenüber, ohne, seit*
- For **motion**: use separable prefix *hin-/her-*, not prepositional adverb: *Sie ging hinein*

### Pronouns of Address
- **du**: children, animals, God, relatives, close friends, students, club members, workmates (blue-collar)
- **ihr**: plural of *du*
- **Sie**: everything else — adult strangers, professional contexts
- Using *du* as social put-down to adults is offensive (and potentially criminal)
- In letters: *Du, Dich, Dein, Ihr, Euch, Euer* may be capitalised (current official practice)

### Special Uses of *es*
- *es* cannot be stressed — use *das* when emphasis is needed: *Das bist du. Das mache ich*
- *es* is omitted in impersonal passive constructions when not in first position: *In dieser Zeit wurde viel gearbeitet*
- 'It's me/you' construction: verb comes **after** *es*: *Du bist es; Ich bin es*
- Dummy *es* (real subject later): verb agrees with the **real subject**, not *es*: *Es liegen zwei Briefe da*
- Anticipatory *es* (before *dass*/infinitive clause) is often omitted — but always retained after *wenn*

## Articles

### Definite Article — Contractions
- Always contracted in speech and writing: *am, beim, im, ins, ans, vom, zum, zur*
- Use **uncontracted** form when: article is stressed; noun is particularised by relative clause; demonstrative force ('that')
- Article must be **repeated** when coordinated nouns differ in gender or number: *das Haus und der Garten*

### Articles with Abstract Nouns
- German uses **definite article** when abstract noun = specific, familiar, definite whole: *Die Zeit vergeht*
- **No article** when abstract noun = vague/partial/new idea (English can insert 'some/any'): *Mut ist nötig*
- **No article** in proverbs and set phrases: *Not kennt kein Gebot*
- Abstract noun + adjective in indefinite/partial sense → no article: *neuer Mut, kleinliche Eifersucht*

### Abstract Nouns in Phrasal Verbs — Key Rules
- Infinitives as nouns with *ins/zum* → **definite article**: *ins Rollen kommen, zum Kochen bringen*
- Feminine nouns with *zu* → **definite article** (*zur*): *zur Verfügung stehen*
- *außer/unter/in* and *haben* + abstract noun → **no article**: *außer Gefahr sein; Angst haben*
- If abstract noun is **qualified by adjective** → article added (even when normally absent)

### Arts, Sciences, Institutions
- Arts/sciences (general): **definite article** in German, not English: *die Literatur, die Geschichte*
- Exception: **no article** for school/university subjects: *in Geschichte, Astronomie studieren*
- Institutions/buildings: definite article: *die Schule, das Parlament, die NATO*
- Exception: no article when proper name is in apposition: *Schloss Sanssouci*

### Languages
- Inflected form (with definite article) = general/typological sense: *Das Spanische ist...*
- Uninflected form (no article or optional) = specific context: *Sie lernt Deutsch; in Deutsch*

## Relative Pronouns

### Core Rule
- Relative pronoun takes **gender/number from antecedent**, **case from its role in the relative clause**
- Relative pronoun can **never be omitted** in German
- Standard relative pronoun: **der** (declines like demonstrative *der*)

### Genitive of *der*: *dessen/deren*
- NEVER declined further: *von dessen Erfolg* (NOT: *von dessem Erfolg*)
- 'One of whom/which': *von denen*: *die Studenten, von denen ich einen kenne*

### *was* as relative pronoun — use after:
- Neuter indefinites: *alles, nichts, etwas, vieles, einiges, folgendes*
- Neuter adjective used as noun (indefinite reference)
- Indefinite demonstrative *das*
- Reference back to a whole clause

### After Prepositions
- Standard (written): **preposition + form of *der***: *die Frau, auf die Sie warten*
- After contexts where *was* is used as relative: **wo(r)+preposition**: *etwas, womit niemand rechnen konnte*
- *wo* for places; *wohin/woher* for motion; *wie* after *die Art*; *warum/weshalb* after *der Grund*

## Indefinites (Key Rules)

### *alle* / *ganz*
- *ganz* is more idiomatic than *alle* with singular nouns: *mein ganzes Geld, den ganzen Tag, ganz Europa*
- *alle* NEVER followed by genitive; following adjective has **weak** endings

### *beide* 'both'
- After definite article or possessive = 'two' (NOT 'both'): *seine beiden Brüder* = 'his two brothers'

### *wer/was* (interrogative/relative)
- *was* not used with most prepositions → **wo(r)+preposition**: *womit, worüber, worauf*
- *wessen* (genitive of *was*) is felt clumsy and avoided in practice
- Idiomatic: German uses **wie** where English uses 'what' with *heißen* and names: *Wie ist Ihr Name?*

## Demonstratives

### Core Rules
- **dieser** (this/these) = primary demonstrative determiner and pronoun; strong endings (like *dieser*-type)
- **jener** (that/those) = formal written German only; in speech use stressed *der/die/das* or *der da*
- **der/die/das** as demonstrative pronoun: same as definite article EXCEPT: genitive = *dessen/deren/derer*; dative pl = *denen*
- *das* (not *es*) used for emphasis/stress: *Das war ich; Das stimmt*
- *derjenige*: always requires a following relative clause; formal style only
- *derselbe* = 'the same' (numerically identical); *der gleiche* = 'of the same kind' (two different instances)
- *solch ein/eine* → more common in speech than inflected *solcher/solche/solches* alone
- *dies* (uninflected) used as subject before *sein* regardless of gender/number of the complement

### Possessives (Determiner vs. Pronoun Endings)
- **As determiner** (before a noun): possessives take *ein*-type endings — nom. sg masc./neut. have NO ending: *mein Bruder, mein Haus*
- **As pronoun** (standing alone): possessives take *dieser*-type endings — nom. sg masc. = *meiner*; nom./acc. sg neut. = *meines*
- *euer-*: drops the inner *e* when an ending beginning with *e* is added: *eure, euren* (NOT *euere*)
- 3rd person ambiguity (*sein-* vs. *ihr-*): use *dessen/deren* (demonstrative genitive) when necessary to clarify whose: *Karl sah Werner und dessen Frau* (Werner's wife, not Karl's)

### Articles with Proper Names
- Personal names: **no article** in standard usage; article + name = colloquial familiarity (especially south German/Austrian)
- Most countries: **no article**; exceptions: *die Schweiz, die Türkei, der Iran, der Irak, der Sudan, die USA, die Niederlande* + all plurals and adjective-containing names
- Mountains, rivers, seas, mountain ranges: **definite article** always
- Cities: **no article**; with attribute: article required (*das heutige Berlin*)

### Articles for Possession (Body Parts/Clothing)
- Use **definite article** (not possessive) with body parts and clothing when the possessor is clear from context (reflexive verb or dative of possession): *Er wäscht sich die Hände; Er klopfte ihr auf die Schulter*

## Adjectives

### Declension Selection
- After der/die/das (and dieser/jener/jeder/welcher/aller/solcher): use **WEAK** — only -e or -en endings
- After ein/kein/possessives: use **MIXED** — strong only in nom m (*ein alter**er** Mann*) and nom/acc n (*ein alte**s** Haus*), weak everywhere else
- After no determiner: use **STRONG** — adjective carries all gender/case marking
- Two or more adjectives: each takes the same ending independently (*mit frischem, kaltem Wasser*)

### Special Forms — Do Not Confuse
- **hoch** stem changes to *hoh-*: *ein hohe**s** Haus* (NEVER *ein hochs Haus*)
- **-el** adjectives always drop the *e*: *dunkel* → *dunklE, dunklER, dunklEM* (NEVER *dunkelER*)
- **-er** adjectives may drop the *e* in speech: *teuer* → *teures Auto* (both written and spoken)

### Nominalized Adjectives
- After *etwas/nichts/viel/allerlei*: strong **neuter**: *etwas Neue**s**, nichts Besondere**s***
- After *alles*: weak: *alles Gut**e**, alles Wichtig**e***
- After *als* ('as a'): usually uninflected in nom sg: *als Erwachsene**r*** (m), *als Erwachsen**e*** (f)

### Adjectives Governing Cases/Prepositions
- **Dative** (not accusative): *ähnlich, bekannt, dankbar, gleich, nahe, schädlich*
- **Genitive**: *bewusst, schuldig, sicher, würdig*
- *interessiert* + **an** + dat (NOT *für*): *interessiert an der Kunst*
- *stolz* + **auf** + acc (NOT *von*): *stolz auf seinen Sohn*
- *neugierig* + **auf** + acc (NOT *an*): *neugierig auf das Ergebnis*
- *zufrieden* + **mit** + dat (NOT *über*): *zufrieden mit dem Ergebnis*

## Adverbs

### Place vs. Direction
- Always distinguish *wo?* (static) vs. *wohin?* (direction): *Er ist oben* vs. *Er geht hinauf*
- *hin* = away from speaker; *her* = toward speaker — never mix them

### Prepositional Adverbs (da/wo compounds)
- For non-personal things: NEVER preposition + *es/das* — use *da(r)-*: *Ich denke daran* (NOT *Ich denke an es*)
- Insert *r* before vowels: *daran, darauf, darüber, darin, darunter* (NOT *daauf, daüber*)
- Questions about things: *woran, worauf, worüber* (not *an was* — colloquial only)

## Comparison

### Core Rules
- Comparative: always **-er** — never use *mehr* + adj to form comparatives
- After comparatives: always **als** (NEVER *wie*): *Er ist größer als ich* (NOT *wie ich*)
- Equality: *so ... wie* (never *so ... als*)

### Irregular Forms — Must Know
- *gut/besser/am besten, viel/mehr/am meisten, gern/lieber/am liebsten*
- *groß/größer/am größten, hoch/höher/am höchsten, nah/näher/am nächsten*
- *wenig/weniger/am wenigsten, bald/eher/am ehesten*

### Superlative
- Predicate/adverb: always *am + -sten*: *am besten, am schönsten*
- Attributive: *der/die/das + -st-* + ending: *der beste, die schönste*
- Stems ending -s, -ß, -z, -d, -t, -sch: insert *e*: *heißeste, kürzeste, kälteste*

## Numerals

### Cardinal Forms — Irregular Shortenings
- *sechzehn* (NOT *sechszehn*), *siebzehn* (NOT *siebenzehn*)
- *sechzig* (NOT *sechszig*), *siebzig* (NOT *siebenzig*)
- Compounds 21–99: units + *und* + tens — *einundzwanzig* (never *zwanzigeins*)
- *eins* in isolation; *ein* before noun: *Gib mir eins* vs. *Gib mir ein Bier*

### Ordinal Formation
- 2–19: **-t-** + ending: *zweite, dritte, vierte ... neunzehnte*
- 20+: **-st-** + ending: *zwanzigste, hundertste*
- Irregulars: **erst-** (1), **dritt-** (3), **siebt-** (7), **acht-** (8 — no added *t*)
- Always write as numeral + period: *am 5. Mai* not *am fünften Mai* (in writing)

## Expressions of Time

### Clock Times
- *halb vier* = **half past three** (NOT half past four) — German *halb* points to the coming hour
- 'At': always *um* + accusative: *um vier Uhr, um halb drei* (NEVER *at four* without *um* in formal writing)
- 'What time is it?': *Wie viel Uhr ist es?* or *Wie spät ist es?*

### Dates
- Day **before** month: *5.6.* = 5th June; *5. Juni* (NOT *Juni 5*)
- Written dates: numeral + period: *am 5. Mai, der 1. Januar*
- 'On the 5th of June': *am fünften Juni* (spoken) / *am 5. Juni* (written)

### Prepositions with Time — Core Rules
- **an** + dat: days of the week and parts of day — *am Montag, am Morgen, am Abend*
- **in** + dat: months, seasons, years — *im Januar, im Sommer, im Jahre 2012*
- Do NOT use bare *in 2012* (English calque) — write *im Jahre 2012* or simply *2012*
- **um** + acc: clock times — *um vier Uhr*
- **zu**: festivals — *zu Weihnachten, zu Ostern, zu Pfingsten*; + *Mal*: *zum ersten Mal*
- **seit** + **present tense** when still ongoing: *Ich warte seit einer Stunde* (I've been waiting for an hour)
- **seit** + **imperfect** when reference point is past: *Ich wartete seit einer Stunde, als er kam*
- Duration in past/future with no present connection: bare **accusative** — *Er blieb einen Monat in Berlin*
- **vor** + dat: ago — *vor einem Jahr* (a year ago)
- **bis zu** + dat when article present: *bis zum Ende, bis zu seinem Tod*; bare *bis* with adverbs/simple phrases: *bis morgen*
- **während** + gen: during — *während der Ferien*; NOT with bare Tag/Abend/Nacht + definite article

### Adverbs of Time — Key Distinctions
- 'Then' (past): *damals*; 'then' (sequence): *dann*; 'then' (questions, intensifying): *denn*
- *dann* CANNOT follow a preposition — use *bis dahin, seitdem, von da an* etc.
- 'Still': *noch*; 'already': *schon*; 'not yet': *noch nicht*; 'no longer': *nicht mehr*
- 'Recently' — choose carefully:
  - *vor kurzem / kürzlich* — a point not long ago, no present connection
  - *neulich* — past point recalled as relevant to now
  - *neuerdings* — something newly started, ongoing up to present
  - *in letzter Zeit* — over a period up to and including now
- *gestern Abend* = last night (before bedtime); *heute Nacht* = tonight/last night (after bedtime) — these do NOT overlap
